Video game regulations is like the Wild West. Few rules and lots of gambling.

1 in every 3 people plays video games regularly.

Most play games for entertainment.

Some use games to connect with others.

Some enjoy the challenge and competition.

Many games let you spend money inside the game.

These are microtransactions.

And this is a loot box.

Suffice to say the video game industry is large, worth more than the film and music industries combined. Revenue is projected to break $400 billion next year, according to market data from Statista. For comparison, the film industry hit $81 billion in 2021.

The way video games make money has been spotlit by researchers and regulators in recent years. Most console or computer games make money just from people buying the game. An increasing number of games, especially mobile games, are adopting a "freemium" revenue model, where the game itself is available for free, but more content, such as new costumes or characters, exists behind paywalls. The strategies a game uses to make money is also known as how it monetizes, or its monetization strategy.

In many cases, a freemium model includes "microtransactions", which are smaller, incremental purchases made within the game. This can look like spending $0.99 for an extra life or $4.99 for a stash of in-game currency. Most games that employ a freemium model also use multiple monetization strategies. One such strategy is called a "loot box", an in-game box that can be purchased either for real world money or in-game currency, and includes a randomized item or set of items. Sometimes, these items have in-game benefits, such as new characters or weapons, or they can be cosmetic, such as new character outfits or emotes.

Every video game is different, which means the game's loot box mechanics can come in many different forms. In Entertainment Arts' "Star Wars: Battlefront II", for example, there were four types of loot boxes that contained different kinds of rewards, all called "crates" in the game.
